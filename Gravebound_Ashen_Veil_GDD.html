Game Design Document

Die, descend, rebuild, and return to a cursed world that never stops opening.

A production-ready design for an original 2D pixel art MMO bullet hell with permadeath, open-world danger, procedural dungeon crawling, Diablo-style loot, and a fair business model.

0. Read First

Assumptions and Design Contract

This document answers the provided brief as a complete starting point for a commercially viable game. It treats "similar to Realm of the Mad God" as genre inspiration only: a fast top-down MMO bullet hell, not a copy of names, art, maps, classes, enemies, items, UI, lore, or proprietary progression.

Primary Decisions

  • Working title: Gravebound: The Ashen Veil. The title is original and can be replaced later without changing systems.
  • Genre: 2D pixel art MMO action RPG, bullet hell, open-world events, instanced dungeon crawling, permadeath, and loot-driven builds.
  • Engine and language: Rust with Bevy for client gameplay, rendering, UI integration, tooling previews, and shared ECS-compatible gameplay modules.
  • Platforms: Browser and PC at launch. PC is the high-fidelity path; browser is a full-featured path with tuned asset and networking budgets.
  • Backend posture: Authoritative, horizontally scalable, service-separated, region-aware, and designed for 10k concurrent players from day one.
  • Business rule: Never sell combat stats, drop-rate boosts, death protection, resurrection, leaderboard advantage, trade advantage, or power acceleration.

Non-Goals

  • No direct recreation of another game's world, gods, class kit names, item names, map layout, art style details, boss patterns, UI, or monetization implementation.
  • No "pay to recover death" mechanic. Death must stay meaningful and trusted.
  • No client-authoritative combat. Every hit, death, drop, trade, and inventory mutation is server-owned.
  • No single-machine MMO prototype architecture. Local development can be simple, but production assumptions must not collapse under scale.

Definition of Fun

The game is fun when a player can read a dangerous screen, make a skillful escape, earn a memorable drop, die with a story worth telling, and start another run within seconds because the next character already has a tempting build path. Addiction comes from mastery and discovery, not manipulation: clear odds, recoverable momentum, social ritual, collection, seasonal goals, and combat that is fair even when it is brutal.

1. Product Overview

Executive Summary

Gravebound: The Ashen Veil is a dark-fantasy MMO action RPG where players create mortal champions sworn to a ruined order, fight through a corrupted open world, dive into dangerous dungeons, and chase build-defining loot. Characters are disposable, stories are permanent, and the account becomes a chapel of dead heroes, trophies, unlocks, cosmetics, crafting knowledge, and seasonal prestige.

CreatePick class, oath, starting kit, cosmetics, and risk profile.
HuntExplore the open world, join events, gather keys, and find portals.
DelveClear dungeons, dodge patterns, defeat bosses, and extract loot.
BuildEquip affixes, craft, store, trade, specialize, and push deeper.
FallDie permanently, preserve legacy value, then reroll faster and wiser.

Commercial Promise

The commercial opportunity is a live-service MMO that is cheaper to content-scale than a 3D MMO, mechanically expressive enough for streamable mastery, and endlessly replayable through seasonal resets, rare item hunting, procedural dungeons, guild objectives, world events, cosmetics, and account collection. The game should feel intense in five minutes, meaningful in one hour, and still dangerous after one thousand hours.

Area Target Design Standard
Session length 5 to 45 minutes, with marathon viability Players can meaningfully progress in short sessions while long sessions create social and loot stories.
Difficulty Fair, lethal, readable Deaths should be attributable to risk, greed, panic, poor preparation, or learning, not invisible damage or network confusion.
Retention Daily, weekly, seasonal, evergreen Layered goals: build chase, dungeon mastery, social contracts, account collection, events, cosmetics, leaderboards.
Monetization Fair but profitable Sell expression and convenience. Provide earnable equivalents for basic account utility. No paid advantage in combat, loot, trade, or rankings.
Scale 10k concurrent players Authoritative Rust backend, interest-managed networking, sharded realms, pooled dungeon instances, region gateways, observability.

2. Creative and System Pillars

Design Pillars

Pillar 1

Every Death Has a Reason

Permadeath is only acceptable if players trust the rules. Damage must be readable, boss patterns must be learnable, latency must be communicated, and disconnect recovery must be explicit.

Pillar 2

Loot Changes How You Play

Rare items are not just higher numbers. They alter ranges, projectile shapes, ability charges, risk rewards, proc conditions, class roles, and dungeon decisions.

Pillar 3

The World Is Shared Pressure

Open-world events create spontaneous cooperation. Other players can save you, lure danger, trigger portals, coordinate events, or inspire greed.

Pillar 4

Fast Back to Fun

Death hurts, but restart friction is low. Character creation, gearing from vault, class presets, training dungeons, and group rejoin flows must be fast.

Pillar 5

Account Legacy Without Paid Power

Long-term progress lives in cosmetics, collection, crafting recipes, titles, achievements, guild projects, and quality-of-life unlocks rather than permanent combat stat creep.

Pillar 6

Server Truth, Client Grace

The server is authoritative, but the client feels immediate through prediction, deterministic pattern rendering, rollback hints, and clear latency UI.

Design Red Lines

  • No random unavoidable deaths. If a bullet is lethal, it must be visually distinct and spatially fair.
  • No paid resurrection, paid stat boost, paid drop boost, paid dungeon completion, paid equipment, or paid leaderboard skip.
  • No mandatory daily chores that punish absence. Daily goals should invite, not threaten.
  • No trading system that lets real-money buyers dominate the economy. Account security, trade limits, logging, and fraud controls are part of the design.
  • No procedural generation that produces unreadable bullet hell arenas, blocked exits, or dead-end dungeon objectives.

3. Players and Platforms

Audience and Platform Strategy

The primary audience is action RPG players who enjoy high-risk loot, MMO social energy, and skill-based combat. The game should also attract bullet hell fans, roguelike players, stream viewers, and players who want an MMO without the time burden of traditional tab-target raids.

Persona Motivation Must Serve
Loot optimizer Buildcraft, affixes, rare drops, trading, min-maxing Deep itemization, transparent ranges, meaningful stat interactions, searchable stash, market tools.
Skill climber Dodging, solo clears, boss mastery, speed records Readable attack language, practice arenas, replays, leaderboards, no paid advantage.
Social wanderer Shared events, guild identity, helping friends, voice-chat nights Easy grouping, low-friction teleport rules, guild halls, pings, assist rewards.
Collector Cosmetics, titles, pets as cosmetics, lore, trophies, account museum Collection UI, seasonal catalogs, earnable prestige, cosmetic previews.
Short-session player Action within minutes, progress without scheduling Fast login, fast reroll, short dungeons, event timers, saved presets.

Platform Requirements

PC Native

  • Primary competitive and high-fidelity client.
  • Supports high refresh rates, lower input latency, larger texture budget, richer effects, better capture tools.
  • Distribution: Steam plus direct launcher as optional future path.
  • Networking: QUIC transport with WebTransport-compatible protocol framing.

Browser

  • Full gameplay path, not a demo, using Bevy WASM build.
  • Asset bundles split by biome and dungeon family.
  • Transport: WebTransport first where supported, secure WebSocket fallback.
  • Input: keyboard and mouse baseline, controller where browser APIs allow.

Content Rating and Safety

Target teen-friendly dark fantasy. Visuals can be grim, haunted, and bloody in tone, but must avoid graphic gore that narrows platform access. Chat, guild names, character names, and trade messages require moderation, reporting, mute, block, and automated filters at launch.

4. Original IP

World, Tone, and Fantasy

The world of Erem is sealed beneath the Ashen Veil, a supernatural dusk created when the last dynasty tried to bind death itself. The attempt broke the cycle of dying. Souls now return as echoes, saints rot into tyrants, cathedrals sink into the earth, and mortal champions join the Gravebound: an order that burns its own names from history so the living can keep fighting.

Fantasy statement: You are not the chosen one. You are one more candle carried into a storm. The account is the order. The character is the flame.

World Premise

  • The Ashen Veil: A metaphysical barrier that keeps souls from passing on and lets dead kingdoms overlap the living world.
  • The Gravebound: A vow-bound order of mortal adventurers who die permanently, but leave relics, maps, rites, and warnings for successors.
  • The Hollow Crown: The broken royal power source that fuels world events, dungeon openings, and seasonal invasions.
  • The Black Reliquaries: Deep dungeons where cursed saints, oathbreakers, and ancient engines guard forbidden loot.
  • The Lantern Halls: Account hub, social space, guild access, vault, crafting, memorials, cosmetics, and seasonal board.

Tone Rules

Do Do Not
Use mournful, dangerous, ritualistic fantasy language. Use parody names, modern slang in lore, or joke-first item names for core content.
Make death part of the world fiction: graves, oaths, bells, relics, ash, candles. Frame death as a monetization opportunity or a cheap punishment.
Let light be precious: lanterns, moonlit fog, saint-gold, fire, pale magic. Overuse neon readability colors outside combat language.
Use corrupted nobility, old churches, plague gardens, bone mines, drowned libraries. Copy any existing MMO realm structure, deity roster, class names, or signature item set.

Regions

Mire of Bells

Low-to-mid danger wetland of sunken chapels, ringing reeds, drowned pilgrims, and bell-tower events that open early dungeons.

The Cinder March

A battlefield of ash storms, buried siege engines, elite patrols, and timed caravan defense events.

Veyr's Orchard

A diseased noble garden where trees grow funeral masks, poison patterns dominate, and rare crafting materials spawn.

Glasscrypt Expanse

High-end desert of mirror tombs, refraction bullets, illusion elites, and hidden dungeon doors.

The Salt Cathedral

Endgame coastline where tides reveal raid portals, enemy density is high, and extraction decisions matter.

Lantern Halls

Non-combat account hub with vaults, wardrobe, guild wings, memorial wall, merchants, crafting, training, and seasonal portals.

5. Game Loop

Core Loop and Retention Loops

Moment-to-Moment Loop

  1. Move through a dense top-down field while reading enemy telegraphs and projectile lanes.
  2. Fire basic attacks manually or with held-fire assist, aim abilities, reposition, and manage cooldowns.
  3. Identify safe pockets, greed windows, ally heals, enemy tells, and exit routes.
  4. Kill enemies, trigger events, collect loot, and choose whether to continue, extract, trade, or delve.

Run Loop

  1. Create or load a character from account roster.
  2. Select realm, guild rally, or dungeon key.
  3. Gain levels quickly through open-world combat and events.
  4. Acquire basic gear, then chase build-defining loot through dungeons and bosses.
  5. Risk increasingly dangerous content for rare drops, cosmetics, crafting recipes, and seasonal score.
  6. Extract to vault or die and convert character history into legacy value.

Account Loop

Legacy

Dead characters contribute memorial score, class mastery, cosmetic title tracks, and unlock visibility. They do not grant permanent combat stats.

Collection

Players fill an account codex with enemies, bosses, item appearances, lore pages, dungeon badges, and seasonal trophies.

Mastery

Practice mode, replays, pattern trainer, and class challenges make skill growth visible without making characters immortal.

Endless Replayability Sources

Layer System Why It Lasts
Mechanical Bullet patterns, class kits, bosses, dodge mastery Players improve personally, not just numerically.
Loot Affixes, rarity, uniques, crafting, build tags Items create build experiments and social trading stories.
World Dynamic events, rotating threats, realm corruption Shared spaces change enough to create daily uncertainty.
Dungeons Procedural layouts, modifiers, keys, bosses, secrets Players can farm known goals but still encounter surprise.
Social Guild halls, rallies, trading, rescues, seasonal pushes Other players are content multipliers.
Live Ops Seasons, events, challenge ladders, cosmetics Fresh constraints and rewards renew the game without wiping identity.

6. Action Design

Combat System

Combat is top-down, real-time, projectile-heavy, and server-authoritative. Skill is expressed through movement, aim, pattern reading, cooldown timing, positioning around allies, and the willingness to stop attacking to survive.

Core Controls

Action Keyboard and Mouse Controller Design Notes
Move WASD Left stick Eight-direction movement with analog smoothing on controller. Movement speed is a primary balance stat.
Aim Mouse cursor Right stick Separate aim and movement are mandatory for skill expression.
Primary fire Hold left mouse Right trigger Held-fire is allowed. Precision weapons still reward aim.
Ability 1 Right mouse Left trigger Class-defining skill. Cooldown, charges, resource, or positioning cost.
Ability 2 Space Left bumper Defensive, movement, support, or risk skill depending on class.
Consumable Q/E/R Face buttons Limited belts. Panic use cannot become pay-to-win.
Extract / Nexus F or dedicated button Menu button hold Emergency exit requires a deliberate input and short lockout rules to prevent abuse.

Combat Model

  • Server tick: 30 Hz authoritative simulation for movement, collision, health, statuses, enemy AI, and loot eligibility.
  • Client render: 60+ FPS with interpolation, predicted own movement, local aim, and deterministic pattern visualization.
  • Projectiles: Enemy bullets are server-authored pattern events with deterministic seeds where possible, reducing bandwidth and improving replay accuracy.
  • Hit detection: Server validates collision using capsule or circle hitboxes, latency grace windows, and cheat-resistant movement reconciliation.
  • Damage clarity: Incoming damage types have stable color, shape, sound, and impact feedback.
  • Risk resource: High-output abilities create self-exposure through immobility, recoil, health cost, delayed recovery, threat generation, or pattern commitment.

Bullet Hell Language

Pattern Type Purpose Readable Form Counterplay
Rings Force distance management Uniform circular spread with visible gaps Pre-position, step through gap, use terrain.
Rotors Test sustained movement rhythm Rotating arms with consistent angular velocity Orbit boss, reverse at safe timings, coordinate group position.
Lanes Control arena zones Long telegraphed strips or volleys Identify lanes, cross only during cooldown, avoid tunnel vision.
Needles Reward precision dodging Thin fast shots with strong contrast and sound Micrododge, stop firing, use defensive ability.
Bloom Create panic and retreat pressure Projectile cluster that expands after delay Read seed point, move early, avoid greedy melee windows.
Memory Reward boss knowledge Sequential safe spots, repeated with variations Learn order, call positions, use map markers.
Personal marks Break group stacking Telegraph attached to selected players Spread, cleanse, bait away, protect low-health allies.

Status Effects

Status effects must be few enough to read and strong enough to matter. Each needs a visual icon, sound, color family, accessibility-safe secondary signal, tooltip, and server-side duration.

Bleed

Damage over time. Mitigated by bandages, guard skills, and vitality affixes.

Frostbind

Reduced movement and turn acceleration. Dangerous but never full control loss in lethal contexts.

Silence

Disables abilities, not movement or extraction. Used sparingly.

Hex

Increases incoming curse damage and alters healing received. Core dark magic debuff.

Guardbreak

Temporary defense reduction after blocking or shield failure.

Radiance

Positive effect that increases healing received and cleanses minor decay.

Exhaustion

Prevents repeated dash or escape chains. Prevents degenerate invulnerability loops.

Marked

Boss targets player with personal pattern. Clear icon plus ground ring.

7. Character Design

Classes, Oaths, and Build Roles

Classes define the player's verbs, not their permanent account identity. Each class has a primary weapon family, two active abilities, a passive, and several oath variants that shift risk and role. Launch should include eight classes, then expand to twelve after core balance stabilizes.

Launch Classes

Class Combat Role Weapons Ability Identity Skill Hook
Ashen Vanguard Frontline bruiser Swords, cleavers, shields Guard arc, short charge, retaliation burst Survive close range without body-blocking allies.
Grave Arbalist Precision ranged Crossbows, repeaters Marked shot, trap nail, piercing line Hold narrow aim lanes under pressure.
Veil Witch Area control caster Hex foci, bone charms Curse zones, delayed blooms, enemy weakening Pre-place damage without trapping allies in danger.
Lantern Saint Support healer Censers, bells Healing pulse, cleanse, light barrier Time support while dodging and avoiding overstacking.
Bloodbound Duelist High-risk assassin Rapiers, knives Dash strike, bleed finisher, health-for-damage Enter lethal space and exit before patterns close.
Relic Mason Summon and utility Hammers, sigil stones Temporary walls, sentry relics, repair wards Shape battlefield without griefing movement.
Mournblade Mid-range lifesteal caster Greatblades, shadow brands Wave slashes, self-heal windows, curse detonations Balance sustain greed against lethal burst risk.
Glass Oracle Pattern manipulation Mirrors, prisms Reflect arcs, decoy image, refracted shots Understand angles and reposition allies safely.

Class Kit Rules

  • Every class has at least one low-cooldown expression tool and one high-impact commitment tool.
  • No class owns a mandatory MMO role. Support helps, but groups cannot require one specific class for normal dungeons.
  • Solo viability is mandatory for leveling and standard dungeons. High-end challenge content can reward specialization.
  • Class power comes from positioning, timing, and item synergy. Passive stat sticks are avoided.
  • Death statistics by class must be monitored to prevent hidden newbie traps.

Oaths

Oaths are class-specific specializations chosen at level thresholds or via gear. They alter abilities without creating permanent account power. Oaths can be respecced with in-game resources, never premium currency only.

Oath of Cinders

Damage-forward variant. Adds burn, recoil, or delayed detonation. Higher output, lower safety.

Oath of Lanterns

Survivability or support variant. Adds shielding, cleanse, or healing tradeoffs. Lower burst.

Oath of Glass

Technical variant. Adds precision, reflections, cooldown resets, or positioning tricks. High mastery ceiling.

8. Shared World

Open World Design

The open world is not a static leveling map. It is a shared pressure field where events, elite patrols, resource nodes, portals, and world state changes pull players into spontaneous cooperation.

Realm Structure

  • Realm capacity: 80 to 120 active players per realm instance depending on region load, event density, and performance telemetry.
  • Realm duration: 2 to 6 hours before climax event and soft reset, with player-triggered acceleration.
  • Safe zone: Lantern Hall is separate. Realms have minor camps but no full safety outside guarded entrances.
  • Danger bands: Outer roads for leveling, midlands for events, deep zones for elite farming, corrupted heartlands for endgame portals.
  • Extraction: Players can return to Lantern Hall with a deliberate input, but certain high-end mechanics impose brief extraction locks after taking major damage or entering boss arenas.

World State

State Variable Source Gameplay Effect
Veil Pressure Time, failed events, elite survival Increases enemy density, improves rare portal chance, raises death risk.
Bell Tolls Major boss kills and realm rituals Announces map-wide events and unlocks timed windows.
Faction Scars Which enemies dominate region events Changes patrols, affix materials, dungeon keys, and hazards.
Grave Heat Player deaths in zone Creates temporary memorial hazards and revenge events.

Open-World Event Types

Ritual Interrupt

Enemies channel at multiple sigils. Players split, clear bullet waves, then converge for a miniboss. Rewards dungeon key fragments.

Funeral Caravan

Escort event with moving safe zones, ambushes, and risk-reward side shrines that spawn stronger waves.

Bell Tower Siege

Map landmark event. Players climb layers of danger, ring bells in sequence, and open a regional dungeon portal.

Wound in the Veil

Short server-wide anomaly with strange projectile rules and unique materials. Designed for streamable surprise.

Executioner Patrol

Roaming elite enemy that chases high-density player groups. Prevents static farming and creates rescue moments.

Fallen Hero Echo

A dead player's memorial can spawn a corrupted echo miniboss using class-themed patterns and carrying partial loot echoes.

9. Dungeon Crawling

Dungeon System

Dungeons are instanced, dangerous, and loot-focused. They provide the strongest Diablo-style progression, but they must preserve bullet hell readability: arenas need room, telegraphs need contrast, and procedural layouts need strict validation.

Dungeon Access

  • World portals: Spawn from events, elites, boss kills, and region states.
  • Keys: Earned through play, crafted, traded, dropped, or occasionally granted through live events. Premium sales of power-relevant keys are not allowed.
  • Guild openings: Guilds can spend earned resources to open scheduled group portals.
  • Practice versions: Low-reward training variants let players learn patterns without risking high-value gear.

Dungeon Tiers

Tier Player Count Time Risk Reward Role
Tier I: Crypts 1 to 10 5 to 8 min Low Leveling gear, first affixes, class basics.
Tier II: Reliquaries 2 to 20 8 to 14 min Medium Rare affixes, crafting mats, first uniques.
Tier III: Black Vaults 4 to 30 12 to 20 min High Endgame gear, build-defining uniques, cosmetics.
Tier IV: Oathbreak Depths 8 to 40 18 to 30 min Extreme Seasonal prestige, mythic crafting, titles, chase cosmetics.
Challenge Echoes Solo or fixed group 3 to 15 min Variable Leaderboards, mastery cosmetics, no tradable power rewards.

Procedural Generation Requirements

  • Layout generator uses authored room templates, connection rules, enemy budget, hazard budget, and boss arena requirements.
  • Every generated dungeon must pass server validation: reachable path, minimum arena sizes, no unavoidable hazard overlaps, spawn safety, exit safety, objective consistency.
  • Room templates are tagged by movement demand: open dodge, lane control, split party, obstacle weave, miniboss, shrine risk, loot rest, lore, secret.
  • Boss arenas are authored, not freeform generated, except for controlled hazard and modifier slots.
  • Dungeon modifiers cannot combine into unfair lethal states. A modifier compatibility matrix is required.

Dungeon Modifiers

Fevered Veil

Enemy patterns gain one extra delayed projectile wave. Reward: higher curse material chance.

Candleless

Reduced minimap visibility and darker rooms. Reward: increased secret room chance.

Glass Floor

Movement inertia slightly increases. Reward: precision affix materials.

Saint's Debt

Healing pulses are reduced during boss phases. Reward: support-oriented unique chance.

Oathfire

Players deal and receive slightly more damage. Reward: faster clear score and extra loot roll.

Restless Dead

Elite enemies can spawn echo adds at low health. Reward: legacy shard drops.

Boss Design Template

  1. Identity: Name, lore role, visual silhouette, arena, music, reward family.
  2. Learning phase: Introduce primary pattern at survivable density.
  3. Pressure phase: Combine primary with movement or add management.
  4. Break phase: Reward damage, healing, or positioning after a successful survival check.
  5. Desperation phase: Faster tempo but no new visual language in the last 20 percent.
  6. Loot moment: Drops are readable, instanced where appropriate, and protected from griefing.

11. Itemization

Diablo-Style Loot System

Loot is the engine of replayability. The item system must support fast readability for action play and deep enough affix math for long-term buildcraft. The guiding rule: items should create decisions, not merely inflate numbers.

Equipment Slots

Weapon

Defines attack pattern, range, cadence, projectile count, and scaling tags.

Offhand

Shield, charm, focus, quiver, relic, or class-specific tool.

Armor

Defense, mobility tradeoffs, resistances, set hooks.

Amulet

Build-defining passive, resource, or utility affixes.

Ring 1

Stat and utility specialization.

Ring 2

Stat and utility specialization with duplicate restrictions.

Belt

Consumable slots, potion behavior, emergency tradeoffs.

Oath Relic

Class variant modifier, late-game build identity.

Affix Families

Offense

Damage, attack cadence, crit window, projectile shape, piercing, area, status application, boss damage with caps.

Defense

Max health, armor, guard, resistances, damage smoothing, emergency barrier, status duration reduction.

Mobility

Movement speed, turn acceleration, dash cooldown, slow resistance, recoil reduction, terrain penalty reduction.

Ability

Cooldown, charges, radius, duration, conditional resets, resource cost changes, oath-specific modifiers.

Utility

Pickup radius, reveal secrets, potion behavior, extraction channel protection, crafting material yield. No combat pay edge.

Risk

Stronger effects that impose health cost, defense loss, delayed self-damage, aggro increase, or movement constraints.

Item Tags

Every weapon, ability, affix, enemy resistance, and crafting material uses tags so the balance team can reason about synergies. Example tags: physical, bleed, curse, radiant, frost, poison, projectile, area, channel, summon, dash, guard, support, boss.

Loot Drop Rules

  • Loot eligibility is based on participation, proximity, encounter contribution, anti-leech checks, and support contribution.
  • Most significant drops are personal or party-instanced to avoid click theft and griefing.
  • Rare tradeable drops are exciting, but key progression cannot require a single impossible market item.
  • Magic find as a paid stat is forbidden. In-game reward quantity modifiers must come from risk modifiers, not store purchases.
  • Drop odds for chase items are visible as rarity bands, with exact rates available where required by platform or regulation.

12. Progression

Character, Account, and Endgame Progression

Character Progression

  • Level range: 1 to 30 at launch. Leveling is fast: 60 to 120 minutes for experienced players, longer for new players.
  • Stat growth: Base class stats grow predictably. Endgame power is primarily gear, skill, and build synergy.
  • Ability unlocks: Core kit by level 10, advanced oath choice by level 20, full build expression by level 30.
  • Death: Character, equipped gear, inventory, active consumables, and unbanked materials are lost or partially converted according to clear rules.
  • Protected items: Account vault items are safe. Equipped cosmetics are account-bound appearances and are not lost.

Permadeath Rules

State Result on Death Reason
Character level and stats Lost Permadeath stakes.
Equipped combat gear Lost, with small memorial salvage chance by item type Risk tension and economy sink.
Inventory loot Lost if not extracted or banked Creates extraction decisions.
Account vault Safe Allows long-term loot chase and recovery.
Cosmetics Safe Purchased and earned identity must persist.
Achievements and codex Safe Account legacy, not combat power.
Season score Recorded at death Death should close a story, not erase recognition.

Account Progression

Memorial Wall

Every level 10+ dead character receives a memorial entry. High-level deaths add epitaph templates, title progress, class mastery banners, and optional replay link.

Codex

Tracks enemies, bosses, lore pages, dungeon completions, item appearances, affix discoveries, and seasonal artifacts.

Crafting Knowledge

Recipes and discovered affix families stay account-wide. Materials remain inventory/vault items and are lost if carried on death.

Cosmetic Wardrobe

Earned and purchased skins, projectile cosmetics, graves, nameplates, guild banners, and hub decorations persist.

Endgame Ladders

  • Dungeon mastery: Clear badges by dungeon, class, modifier, group size, and seasonal rule set.
  • Survival records: Longest life, deepest dungeon tier, boss kills, solo clears, no-hit achievements.
  • Build discovery: Seasonal spotlight rewards for using underplayed affix families and unusual class combinations.
  • Guild progression: Group projects unlock cosmetics, hall wings, banners, and event scheduling tools.
  • Challenge Echoes: Fixed-loadout skill tests where paid storage, gear wealth, and market power do not apply.

13. Economy

Economy, Trading, Crafting, and Sinks

Economy Goals

  • Make found loot exciting even when not personally usable.
  • Support trade without letting the market replace play.
  • Preserve item value through permadeath, crafting sinks, seasonal resets, and binding rules.
  • Limit fraud, botting, real-money trading, price manipulation, and new-player exploitation.
  • Never sell currency, tradeable power, or market advantage for cash.

Currencies

Currency Earned From Spent On Tradeable
Ash Shards Salvage, events, dungeons Basic crafting, repairs in non-combat hub, recipe attempts Limited
Reliquary Seals Bosses, weekly goals Targeted dungeon keys, high-tier crafting No
Guild Tithe Guild activities and contributions Guild hall cosmetics, scheduled portals, social tools No
Crowns Real-money purchase Cosmetics, character slots, vault pages, season pass No
Earned Crowns Seasonal free track, achievements, promotions Selected store items and convenience unlocks No

Crafting

Crafting gives players direction without trivializing drops. It should reduce frustration, let players pursue build goals, and remove items and materials from the economy. Crafting cannot require premium currency.

  1. Salvage: Convert unwanted gear into shards, tag materials, and rare echoes.
  2. Imprint: Save the appearance of found gear into wardrobe if eligible.
  3. Refine: Improve a roll within its tier using matching materials and a cost curve.
  4. Bind: Lock an affix and reroll another at high cost. Creates item sink and chase.
  5. Consecrate: Add a risk affix to an item. Stronger reward, added danger or binding.
  6. Reforge: Full reroll. Expensive, risky, never guaranteed perfect.

Trading

  • Use secure asynchronous market listings plus direct party trade for social exchange.
  • High-risk items can be tradeable, account-bound, season-bound, or bind-on-equip depending on economy role.
  • New accounts have trade limits until trust score increases through playtime, verification, and clean behavior.
  • Market tax, listing fee, crafting sinks, and permadeath item loss regulate inflation.
  • Trade logs are immutable event records for fraud investigation and rollback tooling.

14. MMO Systems

Social, Guild, and Community Design

Social Goals

Players should feel surrounded by danger and opportunity, not forced into rigid schedules. The social design should make help visible, let groups form quickly, and give guilds reasons to care about each member's deaths without turning the game into homework.

Parties

Up to 8 players. Shared pings, health panel, ready check, dungeon join, role tags, and reconnect support.

Raid Bands

Temporary groups up to 40 for world events and Tier IV dungeons. Reduced loot entitlement abuse through participation scoring.

Guilds

Up to 150 members at launch. Halls, scheduled events, banners, permissions, logs, projects, and memorial room.

Communication

  • Text chat: local, party, guild, whisper, trade, system, moderation channel.
  • Context pings: danger, heal here, portal, boss phase, loot, retreat, group here.
  • Emotes: cosmetic and social only. No combat advantage.
  • Optional voice is not launch-critical; integrate platform voice or partner solution later if moderation is ready.
  • Chat must include mute, block, report, profanity filters, spam control, rate limits, and evidence capture.

Guild Projects

15. Business Model

Fair Monetization

The monetization model must be profitable without corrupting trust. The store sells expression, identity, account convenience, optional passes, and social cosmetics. It never sells combat stats, drop boosts, dungeon completion, resurrection, leaderboard advantage, or tradeable power.

Hard Rule

If a purchase makes a character stronger in combat, increases expected loot value, prevents death, improves leaderboard performance, or changes market access in a competitive season, it is not allowed.

Store Categories

Category Examples Fairness Guardrail
Character cosmetics Skins, armor dyes, cloak shapes, death markers, idle poses No hitbox changes, no bullet readability issues, no disguised enemy silhouettes.
Projectile cosmetics Alternate shot visuals and sounds for owned weapons Only local or readability-approved variants; cannot obscure enemy bullets.
Character slots Additional roster slots Baseline includes enough slots for multiple classes. Slots are convenience, and some are earnable.
Vault storage Extra pages, material tabs, loadout tabs Baseline storage must be humane. Seasonal competitive rules equalize key storage where needed.
Wardrobe and profile Nameplates, banners, memorial frames, grave markers Pure expression and status display.
Guild cosmetics Hall decoration packs, banner patterns, trophy plinths Cosmetic only. Guild utility remains earned in game.
Season pass Free and paid cosmetic tracks, earned currency, convenience tokens No paid power. Paid track grants cosmetics and account expression; free track remains rewarding.

Pricing Principles

  • Use transparent prices. Avoid manipulative bundle math and intentionally confusing currency leftovers.
  • Offer direct purchase for major cosmetics instead of relying on randomized loot boxes.
  • Provide earnable store currency through free seasonal tracks and major achievements.
  • Make starter convenience generous enough that non-paying players are welcome community members.
  • Never sell limited-time power FOMO. Cosmetic FOMO must be moderate, with archive rotations over time.

Recommended Launch Offering

Free Account Baseline

  • 4 character slots.
  • Enough vault for normal early and midgame play.
  • Material storage starter tab.
  • Full access to all classes, world content, dungeons, trade, guilds, and seasonal play.

Paid Convenience

  • Additional character slots.
  • Vault pages and loadout tabs.
  • Cosmetic presets and wardrobe organization.
  • Guild hall decoration capacity.

16. Client Architecture

Frontend Client: Rust and Bevy

The client is a Bevy application targeting native PC and WASM/browser. It owns rendering, input, audio, prediction, interpolation, UI presentation, asset streaming, and local accessibility settings. It does not own authoritative combat, inventory, loot, death, trade, or progression.

Prediction Own movement, local shot feel, ability start feedback Final hit results or death outcome.
UI HUD, menus, inventory display, store display, accessibility settings Inventory mutation, purchase grant, trade completion.
Asset Management Manifest loading, bundles, atlases, shader variants, fallback assets Server content rules or drop tables.

  • Use Bevy UI or a Bevy-compatible UI layer for HUD and menus in native PC. Browser shell can host account pages, patch notes, and store pages around the WASM canvas.
  • Maintain strict separation between game HUD and account/store surfaces to reduce performance impact during combat.
  • Sprite atlas budgets must be tiered for browser. High-end PC can use richer animation frames and extra particles.
  • Combat readability wins over visual flourish. Enemy projectiles always render above friendly effects.

17. Server Architecture

Backend Design

The backend is an authoritative Rust service ecosystem. Gameplay simulation runs in shardable realm and dungeon processes. Account, inventory, trade, social, and live-ops systems are separated from real-time simulation so each can scale, deploy, observe, and fail independently.

Service Overview

Service Responsibilities State Model
Gateway Client connections, session validation, routing, rate limits, protocol negotiation, DDoS edge integration Ephemeral with Redis/session cache.
Realm Server Open-world simulation, events, enemies, loot eligibility, portals, deaths Authoritative in-memory simulation with event journal.
Instance Server Dungeons, challenge echoes, boss fights, generated layouts, rewards Authoritative in-memory simulation; durable results at completion/death.
Account Service Accounts, characters, vault, cosmetics, entitlements, purchases, death records PostgreSQL primary, cache for hot reads.
Economy Service Market, trade, crafting, currency ledger, item audit, fraud controls PostgreSQL ledger plus event stream.
Social Service Guilds, parties, chat, friends, block lists, reports, moderation PostgreSQL plus Redis/NATS for presence.
Matchmaker Realm assignment, dungeon placement, queues, region preference, load balancing Ephemeral plus cluster state.
Live Ops Service Feature flags, event schedules, store catalog, season config, announcements Versioned config store with audit.
Telemetry Pipeline Metrics, logs, traces, gameplay events, economy events, anti-cheat signals Stream to warehouse and observability stack.

Authoritative Simulation

  • Server owns entity positions, hitpoints, status effects, projectiles, AI, loot rolls, inventory mutations, death, and instance completion.
  • Client sends input intents, not final positions or hit claims.
  • Simulation uses deterministic formulas in shared crates where helpful, but server result is final.
  • All item grants, trades, purchases, deaths, and vault changes are durable events with idempotency keys.
  • Crash recovery favors consistency over preserving a live fight. Players should not receive duplicated loot or avoid legitimate deaths through process failure.

Recommended Backend Stack

Rust Runtime

  • Tokio for async services.
  • Axum or tonic for HTTP/gRPC internal APIs.
  • bevy_ecs or a headless Bevy schedule for simulation modules where ECS is useful.
  • Quinn or WebTransport-compatible stack for QUIC paths.
  • Serde, postcard, bitcode, or custom binary protocol for compact messages.

Infrastructure

  • Kubernetes for services; game server orchestration with Agones or equivalent fleet manager.
  • PostgreSQL for accounts, ledger, inventory, entitlements, and audit records.
  • Redis for ephemeral presence, matchmaking cache, rate limits, and locks where appropriate.
  • NATS or Kafka for service events, telemetry, economy audit, and live-ops fanout.
  • Object storage and CDN for patches, assets, replays, and logs.

18. Realtime Systems

Networking Model

Networking must preserve bullet hell fairness under browser constraints. The design minimizes per-projectile bandwidth by sending deterministic pattern events, compact snapshots, and interest-managed entity updates.

Transport

Client Preferred Fallback Notes
PC Native QUIC over UDP Secure WebSocket if network blocks UDP Use unreliable datagrams for snapshots where possible, reliable streams for inventory and chat.
Browser WebTransport over HTTP/3 Secure WebSocket Feature-detect at launch. Fallback reduces update rates if needed.

Message Channels

  • Input channel: Client to server, sequence-numbered, high priority, compact action states.
  • Snapshot channel: Server to client, unreliable or latest-only, delta-compressed entity states.
  • Event channel: Reliable ordered events for damage, death, loot, phase changes, chat, inventory confirmation.
  • Pattern channel: Deterministic bullet pattern descriptors with seed, origin, timing, owner, and rules.
  • Control channel: Login, handoff, reconnect, instance join, feature flags, time sync.

Projectile Bandwidth Strategy

Enemy bullet hell can bankrupt bandwidth if each bullet is synchronized as an entity. The server instead emits pattern commands such as "spawn ring pattern at time T with seed S, speed V, radius R, gap pattern G." Clients render bullets deterministically. Server maintains the authoritative collision model and validates hits from the same pattern description. 

PatternEvent {
  server_tick: 1829401,
  pattern_id: "boss.morcant.crown_rotor.03",
  caster_entity: 7719,
  origin: Vec2,
  target_hint: OptionalEntity,
  seed: u64,
  params_hash: u32,
  duration_ticks: 180
}

Latency and Reconnect

  • Show player ping and packet health in a compact HUD indicator, with expanded diagnostics in settings.
  • Support short reconnect windows. Character remains vulnerable for a strict grace policy that prevents disconnect abuse.
  • On disconnect in non-boss realm play, the server attempts safe extraction after a short timeout if the character is not recently damaged or in combat lock.
  • In boss or high-risk locked arenas, disconnect policy must be explicit before entry. Avoid hidden "gotcha" rules.
  • Client records local replay buffer for the last 60 seconds to help support investigate disputed deaths.

Realm Process

  • Target 30 Hz simulation.
  • 80 to 120 players plus enemies and projectiles.
  • Interest grid for entity relevance.
  • Pattern commands over projectile spam.
  • CPU budget alert at 65 percent sustained.

Bandwidth

  • Average target: 6 to 15 KB/s downstream per player during normal play.
  • Spike budget: 25 to 40 KB/s during major boss phases.
  • Upstream target: under 2 KB/s per player.

  • Realm and dungeon processes are stateless enough to be replaced, but authoritative while alive.
  • Matchmaker places players by region, party, guild rally, realm population, skill bracket for training modes, and server health.
  • Hot shard migration is not required for launch; clean realm retirement and replacement is required.
  • Internal APIs use idempotency and durable event journals for inventory, currency, purchases, deaths, and trades.
  • Backpressure exists at login, matchmaking, dungeon creation, market requests, chat, and asset patching.

20. Trust

Security, Anti-Cheat, and Abuse Prevention

Anti-Cheat Philosophy

The most important anti-cheat decision is architectural: the server is authoritative. Client anti-cheat can add signals on native PC, but the design cannot depend on a client being honest, especially with browser play.

Threat Mitigation
Speed hacks Server movement validation, acceleration caps, sequence checks, anomaly scoring.
Aim automation Server cannot fully prevent aim assist, so design uses movement mastery, pattern reading, and anomaly review for impossible tracking.
Packet manipulation Authenticated sessions, message schemas, sequence numbers, rate limits, server-side state validation.
Inventory duplication Ledger-based item events, idempotency keys, transactional grants, single-writer inventory rules.
Real-money trading Trade limits, market monitoring, graph analysis, delayed settlement for risky accounts, enforcement tools.
Bot farming Behavior models, path anomaly detection, captcha only out-of-game if absolutely necessary, reward throttles for suspicious loops.
Chat abuse Filters, rate limits, report tools, mute/block, moderator console, evidence snapshots.
Payment fraud Entitlement ledger, chargeback handling, delayed high-risk gifting, purchase audit, provider risk signals.

Death Dispute Handling

  • Death events log server tick, position, recent damage, status effects, boss pattern events, latency, packet loss, and disconnect state.
  • Support can view a compact replay reconstruction for the final seconds.
  • Automated restoration is rare and limited to verified server faults, rollback events, or documented exploits.
  • Never sell death reversal. Trust in permadeath is a core product asset.

22. Presentation

Art, Animation, VFX, and Audio Direction

Pixel Art Direction

The art style is readable dark fantasy pixel art with restrained palettes, strong silhouettes, and high contrast for combat-critical elements. The world can be grim and textured, but gameplay objects must remain unmistakable at speed.

Asset Guideline Budget Target
Player sprites Distinct class silhouettes with readable weapon profiles and cosmetic attachment rules. 32x32 or 48x48 base with modular overlays.
Enemies Size communicates threat. Elites need immediate silhouette and affix readability. 32x32 to 128x128 depending on role.
Bosses Large silhouettes, clear phase animations, readable attack origins. 128x128 to 256x256 sprite footprint.
Projectiles Enemy bullets outrank friendly VFX. Shape and color indicate damage family. Small sprite set with shader tinting and accessibility variants.
Environments Atmospheric but lower contrast than bullets, enemies, pickups, and exits. Tile chunks and prop atlases per biome.

Combat Readability Palette

  • Enemy lethal: Bone white, blood red, cold violet, and high-contrast gold depending on damage family.
  • Friendly attacks: Lower brightness and thinner shapes than enemy bullets.
  • Healing and safe zones: Lantern gold with icon and ring patterns, not color alone.
  • Poison and curse: Distinct shapes and icons; avoid relying only on green vs red.
  • Loot beams: Rarity color plus shape and sound cue for accessibility.

Audio Direction

  • Music: mournful strings, low drums, chapel bells, distorted choirs, tense percussion during boss phases.
  • Projectile audio: quiet enough to avoid fatigue, but lethal attacks get distinct pre-impact or telegraph sounds.
  • Loot audio: rarity-specific stingers, with volume control and accessibility captions for important drops.
  • Death audio: solemn, immediate, not comedic. Death summary follows quickly.
  • Mix priority: lethal telegraphs, low health, boss phase, party alerts, loot, ambience.
